Social · UX Design

GramCity

A 7-day Google Design Sprint to design an app helping users find the most instagrammable photo locations in any city — from problem definition through competitive analysis, high-fidelity prototyping, usability testing, and iteration.

Role UX Designer & Researcher (Solo)
Duration 1 month
Format Google Design Sprint (7 days)
Tools Sketch, InVision
Deliverables Design concept, Wireframes, User personas, Research documentation

Overview

GramCity helps people find the most instagrammable locations in any city. The insight behind it: "Sometimes we spend too much time looking for the perfect spot to take a picture, that we end up not having enough time to explore." This project was structured as a solo Google Design Sprint — one phase per day, from problem definition to tested prototype.

Day 1 · Define

The core problem: finding photo-worthy locations is time-consuming and often kills the remaining time you have to actually explore. Three "How Might We" questions framed the design challenge:

  • How might we allow users to seamlessly share their favorite photos?
  • How might we allow users to easily find photo examples from other users?
  • How might we allow users to easily find their perfect photo locations, while still leaving them plenty of time to plan the rest of their trip?

User personas

Two personas to represent the core audience: frequent travelers who document their experiences through photos.

Day 2 · Research

Competitive analysis of existing photo-location apps: Explorest, Photomapper, and ShutterSpots. Only Explorest showed meaningful market traction — the others had minimal App Store reviews. This gap confirmed the opportunity.

Day 3 · Decide

Rapid ideation via Crazy 8s, then narrowed to a single direction: a map-driven discovery experience where users can browse and share photo locations, with real examples from other users to set expectations before they visit. A 3-panel storyboard mapped the before/after experience.

Days 4 & 5 · Prototype

Jumped straight to high-fidelity mockups over two days, prioritizing visual polish and a strong UI. Photography is a personal passion, which informed the aesthetic direction — the designs leaned into a clean, photo-first layout with a distinct visual identity.

Day 6 · Test

5 in-person moderated sessions held at a Google micro kitchen. Participants: Peter, Vu, Ben, Michelle, and Julie (ages 26–37) — all SF Bay Area residents, photography enthusiasts, and active Instagram users.

Findings

  • 100% completed all tasks in under 5 minutes
  • 100% asked for camera screens (a missing feature)
  • 80% appreciated the aesthetic and color palette
  • 80% didn't recognize the "New" element as a tappable button
  • 60% were confused about how to follow other user profiles

Day 7 · Iterate

Follow functionality (High severity)

Users couldn't figure out how to follow profiles — there was no clear affordance. Added explicit follow/unfollow buttons to profile pages.

Default category (Medium severity)

Users expected "Popular" to be the default landing state, not "New." Changed the default tab and redesigned the button text layering to make it clearer the element was interactive.

Camera screens (Low severity)

Every participant asked for a camera feature. Added photo capture and camera roll selection screens to round out the core user flow.

Final Designs

Takeaways

UI design and a personal love of photography turned out to be real strengths for this project — the aesthetic resonated with participants immediately. One workflow insight: going directly to high-fidelity skipped lo-fi exploration but enabled faster, more honest feedback from participants who responded to something that felt real.

Next steps: a second round of usability testing to validate the iterations, onboarding screens, and expanded camera functionality.
