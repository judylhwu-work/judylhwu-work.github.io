Consumer · Product Design

BARK App

A solo UX project for a mobile app connecting pup parents to find compatible playmates for their dogs — think Tinder for dogs. Led the full process from discovery research and competitive analysis through two rounds of usability testing and iteration.

Role UX Designer & Researcher (Solo)
Duration 2 months
Tools Sketch, InVision
Deliverables Design concept, Journey maps, User personas, Research documentation

Overview

BARK is an online social app for pup parents to find the perfect playmates for their dogs. Think: Tinder for dogs. Pup parents understand early socialization is important — but also know how hard it can be. Common options (puppy classes, dog parks, daycare) require free time, proximity, and compatibility luck. BARK removes all three barriers.

Problem

Three compounding issues make dog socialization harder than it should be:

  • Lack of free time makes it hard to set up and coordinate playdates
  • Some dogs are picky — compatibility matters, and you often don't know until they meet
  • Limited access to dog parks, classes, or daycare depending on location or budget

Design questions

  • How might we provide a way for pup parents to easily socialize their dogs in a safe, comfortable environment?
  • How might we help users quickly set up playdates for their pups?
  • How might we allow both pup parents and their pups to enjoy the experience?

Phase 1 · Research

Competitive analysis

No existing "doggy Tinder" apps were found — a genuine market gap. I analyzed adjacent apps: Tinder (for the swipe-match mechanic), Kippo (a social-first dating app for gamers), and Dig (a dating app for dog people). Key takeaways informed BARK's core UX patterns.

Screener survey

~200 responses collected from dog parents at Google. The data surfaced a clear demand gap:

  • Only 12.2% were completely satisfied with their current socialization methods
  • 41.1% were actively wanting something better
  • Top values: simplicity, convenience, accessibility, affordability

Interviews

  • Only 30% were content with their current playdate setup process
  • Some had regular "doggy best friends"; others relied on dog parks and hoped for the best
  • 80% said they'd want to use a mobile app for this
  • Main concern: what if the dogs don't get along?

Wireflows

Wireframes helped determine which screens were most essential and allowed experimentation with different layouts before committing to high-fidelity. The wireflow made the end-to-end user journey tangible early.

Phase 3 · Design

Defined the visual language through style guides before moving into high-fidelity screens — establishing typography, color, and component patterns first.

Phase 4 · Test & Iterate

Two rounds of usability testing — in-person at a Google micro kitchen and remotely via Google Hangouts. Participants recruited via email to Dooglers @ Google: pup parents ages 21–45 interested in dog socialization.

Round 1 findings (4 participants)

  • 50% finished in under 5 minutes with no issues
  • 50% wanted more onboarding screens and tutorials
  • 100% wanted an "undo" button on the Explore page

Issue 1 · Explore page missing undo button (High severity)

Inconsistent button treatment between pages left users without a way to reverse an accidental swipe. Added an undo button to the Explore page to match user expectations set by other card-swiping apps.

Issue 2 · Lack of onboarding screens (High severity)

Users unfamiliar with dating apps found the initial flow confusing. Added onboarding tutorials that appear on first use to guide new users through key actions without assuming prior context.

Round 2 results (5 participants)

100% of participants successfully created an account and found a match within 7 minutes. No new issues reported.

Final Designs

Takeaways

  • Two rounds of usability testing is the right cadence — round 1 reveals issues, round 2 validates the fixes
  • Participants catch things you can't see yourself because you already know how it works
  • Making yourself relatable helps participants feel comfortable sharing honest feedback
  • Using an existing community (Dooglers @ Google) made recruitment easy, but limited the participant pool's diversity
  • A well-designed screener survey validates assumptions before you even start designing

Next steps: A/B test the chat screen (owner's name vs. dog's name vs. both), design settings screens for preferences and filters, and integrate Google Maps for in-app directions to playdate locations.
