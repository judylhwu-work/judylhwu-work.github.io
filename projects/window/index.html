Window.

Sole designer on an all-in-one multi-brand shopping platform — from competitive analysis and brand identity through usability testing, resulting in 80% of participants finding the flow clear and intuitive.

Role Sole Designer
Duration 2 months
Team CEO, Senior Software Engineer
Tools Sketch, InVision
Deliverables High fidelity prototypes, Design documents, Research documents

Overview

Window. is an all-in-one online shopping platform with community built in. The core idea: shopping should remember you. Window saves users' tier 1 filters — sizes, favorite brands — so they never have to re-enter them. It surfaces personalized results, lets users shop across multiple brands without tab chaos, and enables checkout within the app itself.

As the sole designer, I worked closely with the client, created the Window logo and brand, and led the project from competitive analysis through usability testing and iteration.

Problem

Shopping has become too time-consuming — users are overwhelmed by options and forced to open multiple websites to browse across brands. The questions driving this project:

  • How might we create an easier way for consumers to shop online?
  • How might we shorten the online shopping process?
  • How might we let consumers shop all their favorite brands without opening multiple websites?

Research

Competitive analysis

I analyzed ShopStyle, Shopbop, and Stitch Fix against Jakob Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics. Key findings:

  • ShopStyle — fails Consistency and Standards (the "more options" icon unexpectedly shows more products, not more menu options) and Aesthetic and Minimalist Design (homepage overloaded with poor information architecture)
  • Shopbop — fails Consistency and Standards (both a heart icon and a separate wishlist icon cause confusion) and Aesthetic and Minimalist Design (jam-packed homepage with endless scrolling)
  • Stitch Fix — passes all 10 heuristics; used as the benchmark for best-in-class UX

Product audit

The audit identified three core usability weaknesses in the initial prototype: poor information architecture, no "show all products" option, and a lack of distinct branding. Strengths included the brand card layout, in-app checkout, and additional filters above results.

User personas

Two primary personas defined to represent Window's target users — millennial women who shop online across multiple brands.

Usability Testing

Remote moderated testing with 5 millennial women (late 20s–early 30s) who shop online. Conducted via Google Hangouts during COVID-19.

Strengths identified

  • 100% loved the overall feel — clean, minimalistic, and modern
  • 80% found the user flow clear and intuitive
  • Positive feedback on visual branding, product photo layout, and brand tag consistency

Issues identified

  • 40% found brand tags hard to read due to low contrast (white text on yellow)
  • 40% found the login screens unnecessary (three screens felt excessive)
  • 40% wanted to see tier 2 filters (users were confused about what filtering options existed)
  • 20% wanted more contrast in login screens
  • 20% wanted customer product reviews
  • 20% felt search needed more visual emphasis on the main page

Iteration

Seven usability issues were identified and resolved across two rounds of iteration:

  • Login contrast: Changed text from white to black and brightened background photos — high severity
  • Brand tag contrast: Changed tag text from white to black against yellow background — high severity
  • Customer reviews: Added a third product card for real customer reviews — medium severity
  • Brand window sizing: Reduced brand window size to draw less attention and emphasize search — low severity
  • Login flow: Condensed three login screens into two by removing an unnecessary description page — low severity
  • Tier 2 filters: Created a tier 2 filters screen (occasion, colors, care instructions, etc.) — low severity
  • Indicator dots: Increased contrast on product page indicator dots — low severity

Final Designs

Takeaways

This project underscored how much usability testing surfaces that even user-centered design misses. Designing with myself as the user wasn't enough — testing quickly revealed gaps I hadn't anticipated. I also gained a deep appreciation for client communication and trust: with significant creative freedom, I was able to own the brand direction fully, which made the collaboration unusually rewarding.

Next steps I scoped for the client: a second round of usability testing to verify all fixes, and a full onboarding flow covering sign-up and tutorials.
